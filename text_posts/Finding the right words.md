I miss him, but not in the sentimental way. In the literal sense: he is missing from me.

Sometimes, in our home in suburbia Georgia, I would come down stairs late at night for a drink, and I would find him sitting by himself in the corner of the kitchen. The lights would be off. He wears the darkness like a blanket. I know he is confronting the imminence of his mortality, made worse by my being there because it reminds him of what he'll soon leave behind. I try to find the words -- I always try. But they never reach me, and I never reach him. In those moments, I wished somehow to cross that insurmountable distance that separates two souls, if only I could find the words.

Instead, I linger for a moment longer than was necessary. I didn't really need a glass of water. I wanted to be in his presence for a moment longer, that was all. I don't know if he even knew that I knew he was sitting in the dark corner of the kitchen, in that sorry state. I don't know if he could feel that I was sorry, too.

That reminds me of an afternoon we shared together, in the backyard. We sat on the bench he built all those years ago. I would leave for the airport in a few minutes, and we sat with the weight of goodbye heavy in the summer air. I said, "I love you very much" but I couldn't look at him. In the corner of my eye, I saw that he couldn't look at me either. He sent me off with a quiet wave. I would later hear from mom when she came home later that evening, that he had been crying. "I couldn't say it back," he told her.

But I heard it anyways; I would've known even if mom had not told me. And I know it even better knowing that he couldn't have said it then. I hope that was what he heard all those times I found him alone in the kitchen. I hope he heard all the words I couldn't say, didn't know how to say, or simply couldn't bear to say. Somehow they come out anyways.
